# Key terms

The Talmud's study hall runs on a shared vocabulary, most of it Hebrew or Aramaic. This is a plain-English guide to the terms you will meet in the app and on the page.

## The text and its parts

**דַּף / עַמּוּד** (daf / amud): A *daf* is a folio, a leaf of the Talmud with two sides. Each side is an *amud*, designated aleph (א) or bet (ב). Daf 2a is the front side of the second leaf; 2b is the back. The tractates start at daf 2, by convention.

**מִשְׁנָה** (Mishnah): The first layer of the Talmud, a codification of the Oral Law compiled by Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi around 200 CE. The Mishnah states rules in brief, often setting two or more opinions against each other. Each sugya (see below) typically opens with the relevant Mishnah.

**גְּמָרָא** (Gemara): The second layer, the rabbinic discussion and elaboration of the Mishnah, compiled in Babylon over several centuries and closed around 500 CE. The Gemara is what most people mean when they say "the Talmud." It questions the Mishnah, raises contradictions, and follows the argument wherever it leads.

**סוּגְיָה** (sugya): The running argument on a single legal or conceptual topic. A sugya begins with a Mishnah or a problem and works through objections, proposed answers, new objections, and resolutions. Knowing the shape of a sugya is the skill that makes a page navigable.

## The argument's moves

**קֻשְׁיָה** (kushya): A difficulty or objection raised against a reading or a ruling. The Gemara raises kushyot constantly, often from another tractate or another tradition. Feeling the difficulty before the Gemara names it is a sign that you have understood the page.

**תֵּירוּץ** (teirutz): A resolution. The teirutz answers the kushya, though it may generate a fresh kushya of its own. Not every teirutz holds.

**תֵּיקוּ** (teiku): The question stands unresolved. A teiku is an honest acknowledgment that the Talmud could not find a satisfying resolution. Some questions have stood in teiku for fifteen centuries. The word is taken by some to be an acronym and by others to be the Aramaic for "it stands"; both readings say the same thing.

**שַׁקְלָא וְטַרְיָא** (shakla ve-tarya): The give and take of Talmudic argument, literally the taking and the carrying. The phrase names both the structure of the text and the practice of chevruta study. The students' back-and-forth mirrors what the editors of the Talmud did across generations.

## Study partners and places

**בֵּית מִדְרָשׁ** (beit midrash): The house of study, the room where Torah is learned, usually adjacent to a synagogue. A working beit midrash is loud: pairs reading aloud, translating, objecting, repeating.

**חַבְרוּתָא** (chevruta): A study partner, or the partnership itself. The word comes from the Aramaic root for companionship. Chevruta study puts two people on one text, each obligated to press the other.

**שִׁיעוּר** (shiur): A lesson given by a teacher or advanced learner. A maggid shiur (מַגִּיד שִׁיעוּר) leads the session. Chevruta preparation before a shiur and review afterward are both part of the method.

## The commentators on the page

**רַשִׁ"י** (Rashi): Rabbi Shlomo Yitzhaki (1040 to 1105), whose commentary runs in a distinctive script down the inner margin of the Vilna-edition page. Rashi gives the plain sense: what each word means, how to parse a difficult construction. He is the first stop when you are stuck.

**תּוֹסָפוֹת** (Tosafot): The Additions, written by the school of Rashi's grandsons and their students in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, running down the outer margin. Tosafot raises the hard questions, noting where the passage conflicts with another tractate or where Rashi's reading creates a problem.

**רִאשׁוֹנִים / אַחֲרוֹנִים** (rishonim / acharonim): The earlier authorities (roughly the eleventh to fifteenth centuries) and the later authorities (from the sixteenth century onward). A learner tracing how a ruling emerges from a sugya moves outward through these layers after Rashi and Tosafot.

## Modes and orientations

**דַּף יוֹמִי** (daf yomi): The daily page, the worldwide discipline of studying one folio of the Babylonian Talmud each day. One page a day, the same page for every learner worldwide, completing the entire Talmud in about seven and a half years.

**בְּקִיאוּת** (bekiut): Breadth. The mode of covering ground, moving forward, accepting that the second cycle will show you more than the first. Daf yomi is bekiut.

**עִיּוּן** (iyun): Depth. Slow, intensive analysis of a single sugya, every word questioned, every parallel tracked.

**חֲזָרָה** (chazara): Review and repetition. Chazara is not what comes after learning; it is part of how learning happens. The tradition insists on it.

**לִשְׁמָהּ** (lishmah): For its own sake. Study taken up because the text demands engagement, not for reputation or credential. What lishmah means shows up less in what the student says about his motives and more in what the study does to him.

## The structure of the text

**צוּרַת הַדַּף** (tsurat ha-daf): The form, or layout, of the printed page. The Gemara text sits in the center column, with Rashi in the inner margin and Tosafot in the outer. The layout is a guide to the order of study.

## The tradition's terms for dispute

**מַחֲלֹקֶת לְשֵׁם שָׁמַיִם** (machloket l'shem shamayim): A dispute for the sake of Heaven. Avot 5:17 sets this against a dispute for its own sake. An argument that has the truth as its object is one that can end in resolution or acknowledged disagreement, and still leave both parties the better for it.

**אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ** (eilu va-eilu): Both these and those are the words of the living G-d. The phrase from Eruvin 13b holds that the positions of both the School of Shammai and the School of Hillel are, in some sense, correct, even when they contradict. The Talmud can hold the dispute open because it trusts that both sides were engaged in the same serious effort.
