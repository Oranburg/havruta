# How the daf is learned

The Talmud is not learned the way most books are read. There is a method, worked out over many centuries, and once you can see it the page stops looking like a wall of text and starts looking like a conversation you can join. This page walks through how the daf is actually studied, in the בֵּית מִדְרָשׁ (*beit midrash*, the house of study) and in the daily discipline of דַּף יוֹמִי (*daf yomi*, the daily page).

## The beit midrash is loud

Walk into a working beit midrash for the first time and the noise is the first thing you notice. It sounds like an argument, because it is one. Pairs of students talk at once, reading aloud, translating, objecting, repeating. No one reads silently. No one sits alone with a text and a highlighter. The room is a shared spoken event.

This follows from a conviction the tradition states openly: Torah lives in the mouth, not only in the eye. The Rabbis read it out of the word וְשִׁנַּנְתָּם (*ve-shinnantam*, "you shall teach them diligently," Deuteronomy 6:7). The Talmud in Kiddushin 30a takes the root to mean not only diligence but sharpening:

<blockquote className="hebrew-block">

תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְשִׁנַּנְתָּם״, שֶׁיְּהוּ דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה מְחוּדָּדִים בְּפִיךְ, שֶׁאִם יִשְׁאַל לְךָ אָדָם דָּבָר אַל תְּגַמְגֵּם וְתֹאמַר לוֹ, אֶלָּא אֱמוֹר לוֹ מִיָּד

</blockquote>

> The Sages taught: "And you shall teach them diligently [*veshinnantam*]" — that matters of Torah should be sharp and clear in your mouth, so that if a person asks you a word of Torah, you should not stammer and then tell him; rather, tell him immediately.

The phrase the passage turns on is דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה מְחוּדָּדִים בְּפִיךְ (*divrei Torah meḥuddadim be-fikha*, words of Torah sharpened in your mouth). The mouth is the organ of learning. Sharpness comes from speaking, repeating, and arguing until the word sits ready on the tongue. What sits only on the page is not yet learned in the tradition's sense.

Proverbs gives the image the room is built around:

<blockquote className="hebrew-block">

בַּרְזֶ֣ל בְּבַרְזֶ֣ל יָ֑חַד וְ֝אִ֗ישׁ יַ֣חַד פְּנֵֽי־רֵעֵֽהוּ׃

</blockquote>

> As iron sharpens iron, so a man sharpens the face of his friend.

The same passage in Kiddushin applies this verse to study partners directly: people sharpen each other. The sharpening needs friction, the friction needs two people, and two people need speech.

## The chevruta pair: how two people work a line

A חַבְרוּתָא (*chavruta*, a study pair, from the Aramaic root for companionship) works through the text together, out loud. The method has a recognizable rhythm even though no single text writes it down as a set of steps.

One partner reads the line aloud. Reading aloud forces a pronunciation, a pronunciation forces a parsing, and a parsing forces a meaning. The other partner translates or paraphrases what was read, which is the first test: a line you cannot render is a line you have not yet understood. Then the one who read proposes what the line means, what problem it answers, what claim it advances. The other objects. The objection is the center of the method. A partner who says "yes, that makes sense" has not done the job. The job is to ask whether the reading accounts for this word, whether it conflicts with the next passage, whether Rashi supports it or cuts against it. A proposal has to survive challenge before the pair moves on. The response is either a defense of the reading or a revision of it, and the pair stays on the line until they have wrung some resolution from it, even when the resolution is that they do not know, and here is exactly why.

This back and forth is what the tradition calls שַׁקְלָא וְטַרְיָא (*shakla ve-tarya*, the taking and the carrying). The phrase names both the work of the pair and the inner structure of the Talmudic text itself. The students are doing in miniature what the Talmud's editors did across generations.

The discipline that makes it work is arguing toward the text rather than at each other. A chevruta is a joint project of figuring out what the page says, not a contest about who is smarter. When the objection lands, the right move is to go back to the words and look again. The text is the third partner at the table, and it has the last word.

## The page is built as question and answer

The printed page is itself a record of this method applied over centuries, and seeing that makes it navigable.

A סוּגְיָה (*sugya*, the running argument on a single legal topic) usually opens with a Mishnah, which states a rule or sets two positions against each other. The Gemara then asks: who taught this Mishnah, what is the reasoning, does it conflict with another teaching. Before reaching for any commentary, the student's task is to feel the difficulty the Gemara is about to raise.

A few terms name the movement of the argument. קֻשְׁיָה (*kushya*, a difficulty or objection) is what the Gemara raises against a proposed reading, whether a contradiction from another source, a logical problem, or an apparent impossibility. תֵּירוּץ (*teirutz*, a resolution) is the answer offered, and it does not always hold; it can generate a fresh difficulty. תֵּיקוּ (*teiku*) is the signal that the question stands open. The Gemara does not force a resolution it cannot honestly reach, and some of these questions have stood unresolved for fifteen centuries. A teiku is an honest marker, not a placeholder for a missing answer.

Reading a sugya means tracking this shape: what is the difficulty, what is the proposed answer, what does the answer generate, where does the chain end. A student who can state the structure of a sugya in plain prose has understood it. A student who can only recite the words has not.

## The order of approach to the page

The physical page of the Vilna Talmud, the edition printed since the 1880s that became the worldwide standard, arranges its commentary around the central text in space. This layout, called צוּרַת הַדַּף (*tsurat ha-daf*, the form of the page), is a guide to the order of study.

The Gemara text sits in the center column, and the student reads it first, line by line in the order it appears. רַשִׁ"י (*Rashi*, Rabbi Shlomo Yitzhaki, 1040 to 1105) runs in a distinctive script down the inner margin and gives the plain sense: what each word means, what problem the passage addresses, how to parse a hard construction. When you are stuck on what a passage means at the most basic level, Rashi is the first stop, and his commentary was written to be read alongside the text, a line at a time, not after the page is finished.

תּוֹסָפוֹת (*Tosafot*, the Additions) run down the outer margin, written by the school of Rashi's grandsons and their students across the twelfth and thirteenth centuries. Tosafot raises the hard questions: where the passage seems to contradict another, where Rashi's reading creates a problem, where the legal stakes run deeper than the surface suggests. Rashi teaches you what the page says; Tosafot teaches you why it is difficult. Moving from Rashi to Tosafot is moving from reading to analysis.

Beyond these two, the page points to the רִאשׁוֹנִים (*rishonim*, the early authorities, roughly the eleventh to fifteenth centuries) and the אַחֲרוֹנִים (*acharonim*, the later authorities, from the sixteenth century to the present), and to the codes, chiefly the Shulchan Aruch of Rabbi Yosef Karo (1563) and the Mishneh Torah of Rambam. A student tracing how a ruling emerges from the sugya moves outward through these layers. That is the work of advanced study, not the starting point.

A typical daf yomi learner covers the Gemara text with Rashi, turns to Tosafot where the teacher or the partner flags a real difficulty, and does not exhaust every rishon on a single page. That depth belongs to a different mode of study.

## Breadth and depth: two ways of learning

The tradition keeps two honest postures toward the text, each with its own purpose.

בְּקִיאוּת (*bekiut*, breadth and fluency) is the mode of covering ground. The daf yomi practitioner is doing bekiut: one page a day, forward motion, the aim of passing through the whole Talmud. In bekiut you read, translate, follow the main argument of the sugya, and move on. You do not stop to resolve every difficulty. You accept that the second cycle will show you more than the first, and the third more than the second.

עִיּוּן (*iyun*, depth) is slow, intensive analysis of a single sugya. In iyun a pair might spend an entire session, two or three hours, on half a page. Every word is questioned, every relevant rishon consulted, the logical structure mapped, contradictions with parallel passages tracked. Iyun is what yeshiva students do in their main learning block.

Neither mode is the superior one, and the tradition needs both. Breadth without depth gives broad familiarity and shallow roots. Depth without breadth gives deep command of one passage while leaving the student unable to place it in the larger movement of the Talmud. The great scholars did both: wide fluency in the whole and deep mastery of particular tractates. For daf yomi the daily discipline is breadth by design. The page moves forward each day whether or not yesterday's passage was fully settled, and the learner slowly comes to tell what can be carried forward as an open question from what has to be settled before moving on.

## Review is part of learning

חֲזָרָה (*chazara*, review and repetition) is not what you do after you have learned something. It is part of how you learn it, and the tradition is emphatic on the point.

The Gemara in Eruvin 54b records how Moshe transmitted the Oral Law:

<blockquote className="hebrew-block">

תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, כֵּיצַד סֵדֶר מִשְׁנָה: מֹשֶׁה לָמַד מִפִּי הַגְּבוּרָה. נִכְנַס אַהֲרֹן, וְשָׁנָה לוֹ מֹשֶׁה פִּירְקוֹ, נִסְתַּלֵּק אַהֲרֹן וְיָשַׁב לִשְׂמֹאל מֹשֶׁה. נִכְנְסוּ בָּנָיו, וְשָׁנָה לָהֶן מֹשֶׁה פִּירְקָן. נִסְתַּלְּקוּ בָּנָיו, אֶלְעָזָר יָשַׁב לִימִין מֹשֶׁה, וְאִיתָמָר לִשְׂמֹאל אַהֲרֹן... נִמְצָא בְּיַד הַכֹּל אַרְבָּעָה.

</blockquote>

> The Sages taught: What was the order of the Mishna? Moses learned from the mouth of the Almighty. Aaron entered and Moses taught him his lesson. Aaron moved aside and sat to Moses' left. His sons entered and Moses taught them their lesson. His sons moved aside; Elazar sat to Moses' right and Itamar to Aaron's left... Hence everyone had heard it four times.

From this account Rabbi Eliezer draws a rule:

<blockquote className="hebrew-block">

מִכָּאן אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: חַיָּיב אָדָם לִשְׁנוֹת לְתַלְמִידוֹ אַרְבָּעָה פְּעָמִים.

</blockquote>

> From here Rabbi Eliezer said: A person is obligated to teach his student four times.

The same tractate tells of Rabbi Perida:

<blockquote className="hebrew-block">

רַבִּי פְּרִידָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָהוּא תַּלְמִידָא דַּהֲוָה תָּנֵי לֵיהּ אַרְבַּע מְאָה זִימְנֵי וְגָמַר.

</blockquote>

> Rabbi Perida had a certain student whom he would teach four hundred times before he learned the material.

When Rabbi Perida was distracted during one session and the student could not absorb the lesson, he taught it again: four hundred more times. The point is not that the student was slow. The point is that teaching four hundred times, and doing it again when the conditions were wrong the first time, was the right thing to do. Repetition until the material sits securely is the method.

In daf yomi the review is built into the cycle. Because the same page comes around again in roughly 2,700 days, the learner who does not fully grasp a passage will meet it again. The discipline is to arrive each time carrying what you learned before and to press a little further. The Talmud opens differently on the second pass, and that is how the tradition means the learning to deepen.

## The teacher and the shiur

A שִׁיעוּר (*shiur*, a lesson) is a session led by a מַגִּיד שִׁיעוּר (*maggid shiur*, the one who gives the lesson), a teacher or sufficiently learned senior student who walks a group through the daf. The shiur usually comes before or after the chevruta session, not instead of it.

The relationship between the two runs in both directions, preparation and review. A pair prepares for a shiur by working through the text beforehand, so that when the teacher raises a difficulty the students have already felt it, and the shiur lands harder for it. Afterward the pair returns to the text to weigh what they heard against what they understood.

The principle from Kiddushin 30a applies here too:

<blockquote className="hebrew-block">

לְעוֹלָם יְשַׁלֵּשׁ אָדָם שְׁנוֹתָיו: שְׁלִישׁ בַּמִּקְרָא, שְׁלִישׁ בַּמִּשְׁנָה, שְׁלִישׁ בַּתַּלְמוּד.

</blockquote>

> A person should always divide his years into thirds: a third for Bible, a third for Mishna, and a third for Talmud.

The Gemara immediately asks who knows how long one will live, and answers that the division is by days, not years: each day of study should touch all three layers. A well-run shiur sets the Talmudic passage inside that wider structure, connecting the sugya to the Mishnah it addresses and, where it matters, to the biblical sources beneath both.

A teacher who explains everything produces students who understood that one explanation. A teacher who poses the difficulty precisely and then asks the students to find the resolution produces students who can face the next page on their own. The aim in the beit midrash is a student who has so taken in the method that the teacher becomes unnecessary, not because the teacher was dispensable, but because the student has made the method his own.

## Study that forms the person

The purpose of all this effort is not the accumulation of legal knowledge. The tradition's term for the right orientation is לִשְׁמָהּ (*lishmah*, for its own sake): study taken up because the text demands engagement, not because the student wants a credential or a reputation.

What lishmah means shows up less in what the student says about his motives and more in what the study does to him. The passage in Avot 6:1 lists what lishmah study produces, and the list is character: humility, patience, reverence, distance from pride. A student who leaves a session with a sharper argument and an unchanged character has missed what the learning was for.

This is why the social shape of the beit midrash matters. The mutual challenge of the pair, the shiur's demand that you arrive prepared, the return that review requires: each of these works on the person, not only on the information in the person's head. You cannot do chevruta while performing, because the partner sees through it. You cannot review without discovering what you did not actually keep. The practices are built to keep the student from fooling himself about how much he has learned.

Daf yomi adds a daily commitment on top. The page moves forward every day. The learner who missed yesterday is behind, and knows it. The one who shows up today shows up as someone who chose to. Over seven and a half years, that accumulation of daily choices is itself a formation.

## Daf yomi: one page, the whole world, every day

דַּף יוֹמִי (*daf yomi*, the daily page) is the worldwide discipline of studying one folio of the Babylonian Talmud each day, completing all 2,711 folios in about seven and a half years. Rabbi Meir Shapiro of Lublin proposed it at the First World Agudath Israel Congress in Vienna in 1923, as a project for worldwide Jewish unity: Jews everywhere, whatever their background or location, would study the same page on the same day. The current cycle, the fifteenth, is running now. The fourteenth concluded with a Siyum HaShas (*siyum*, completion; *hashas*, the six orders of the Talmud) at MetLife Stadium in January 2020, attended by roughly ninety thousand people, with parallel events around the world.

The daily dimension changes what the learner is doing. He is not studying Talmud in general; he is studying this page, today, alongside every other daf yomi learner on earth. The page is a shared object. When a learner works a kushya with his partner, the same kushya is being raised in Jerusalem and Buenos Aires and Melbourne. The shiur his teacher gives tonight, thousands of teachers are giving in parallel. The Talmud's own insistence that study is communal, that it is acquired בַּחֲבוּרָה (*ba-ḥavruta*, in fellowship, as Berakhot 63b says), becomes visible in daf yomi in a way private study cannot match.

The app places you inside that community, even when you study alone.
