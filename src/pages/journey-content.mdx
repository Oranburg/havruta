דַּף יוֹמִי (*daf yomi*, the daily page) is a worldwide commitment: one folio of the Babylonian Talmud studied each day, the same folio for every learner everywhere on earth. The cycle covers all 2,711 folios of the Talmud and takes about seven and a half years to complete.

Rabbi Meir Shapiro of Lublin proposed the practice in 1923, at the First World Agudath Israel Congress in Vienna, as a project for worldwide Jewish unity. The idea was simple: wherever a Jew happened to be, whatever city or continent, whatever his community or background, he and every other learner would be on the same page that day.

## The six orders

The Babylonian Talmud is arranged in six orders, called סְדָרִים (*sedarim*, orders; singular סֵדֶר, *seder*). Each seder contains several tractates (מַסֶּכְתּוֹת, *masekhtot*), each addressing a domain of Jewish law and life.

Not every tractate in every order has a Gemara: Zeraim and Tahorot are largely Mishnah only in the Babylonian Talmud, with the exception of Berakhot in Zeraim. The daf yomi cycle follows the tractates that have Gemara.

## Siyum HaShas

When the cycle ends, learners gather for a סִיּוּם הַשַּׁ"ס (*Siyum HaShas*, the completion of the six orders). The fourteenth cycle concluded in January 2020 with a celebration at MetLife Stadium attended by roughly ninety thousand people, with parallel events in cities around the world. Then the fifteenth cycle began, on the same day, with Berakhot 2a.

The Siyum is both a celebration and a beginning. The tradition has a custom of reading the last words of the Talmud and then the first words on the same day, because the Torah is not something you finish, only a place you return from.

## Where you are in the cycle

The [Shas map](/shas) shows every tractate and your progress through the current cycle. Each day's folio is marked as you study it, and the map gives you a sense of where the cycle stands relative to the whole.

The current cycle is the fifteenth. Whatever day you start, the page you study today is the page a learner in Jerusalem and a learner in Buenos Aires and a learner on a train in Chicago is also studying. That shared object is what Rabbi Shapiro was describing in 1923, and it is still what makes daf yomi more than a personal reading project.
